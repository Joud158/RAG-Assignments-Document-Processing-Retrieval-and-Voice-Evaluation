# Chapter 3 — Soil Testing, Fertility, and Fertigation

## 3.1 Start with a soil test
A soil test prevents guessing. Key values:
- pH (affects nutrient availability)
- Organic matter (buffering and water holding)
- Electrical conductivity (salinity)
- N, P, K levels; plus micronutrients (Fe, Zn, B)

## 3.2 Interpreting common patterns
- Low organic matter: nutrients leach quickly; split applications work better.
- High pH (alkaline): iron and zinc become less available; chlorosis can appear on young leaves.
- Very high phosphorus: can reduce zinc uptake.

## 3.3 N-P-K basics (simple view)
- Nitrogen (N): vegetative growth, leaf color; too much causes soft growth and disease risk.
- Phosphorus (P): root growth and early vigor; important early.
- Potassium (K): fruit quality, stress tolerance, water regulation.

## 3.4 Split application strategy (why it helps)
Instead of one big fertilizer dose, split into smaller weekly doses. Benefits:
- Less leaching, especially in sandy soils.
- More stable growth.
- Easier to correct if plants respond badly.

## 3.5 Example program: potatoes (moderate yield target)
Assume low organic matter and irrigated field.
1) At planting
   - Provide a balanced starter (includes P) near the seed piece.
2) Early growth (2–4 weeks)
   - Nitrogen in small weekly doses to build canopy.
3) Tuber initiation
   - Keep nitrogen steady but not excessive (too much delays tuber formation).
   - Increase potassium to support tuber bulking.
4) Bulking
   - Prioritize potassium; monitor leaf color and avoid late nitrogen spikes.

Rule of thumb decision:
- If leaves are pale from bottom up: increase N slightly.
- If leaf edges scorch and plants look thirsty even when irrigated: check salinity and potassium.

## 3.6 Micronutrients quick notes
- Iron deficiency: yellow young leaves with green veins (common in high pH).
- Boron: affects flowering and fruit set; both deficiency and excess are harmful.
- Calcium: related to blossom-end rot in tomato; consistency of irrigation matters more than simply adding calcium.

## 3.7 Fertigation (fertilizer through drip)
Fertigation is efficient because nutrients go where roots are. Best practices:
- Filter water to avoid clogging.
- Inject in the middle of the irrigation cycle, then flush with clean water.
- Use smaller frequent doses rather than rare large doses.

## 3.8 Nutrient budgeting
Track:
- fertilizer type, amount, and cost
- expected yield
- price per kg
This links agronomy decisions to business decisions.
