# Chapter 2 — Irrigation Scheduling, Soil, and Water Quality

## 2.1 The goal
Irrigation should keep water in the root zone without waterlogging or salt buildup. Too little water reduces growth; too much water causes root diseases, nutrient leaching, and wasted pumping cost.

## 2.2 Soil texture changes everything
Sandy soils:
- Low water-holding capacity; water moves fast.
- Best practice: shorter, more frequent irrigations (e.g., daily or every 2 days in hot periods).

Loamy soils:
- Balanced holding capacity; moderate frequency.

Clay soils:
- High holding, slow infiltration; longer but less frequent irrigation; avoid saturating because roots need oxygen.

## 2.3 Practical scheduling without fancy tools
Use a simple “bucket” view of the root zone:
- Root depth (vegetables): often 20–40 cm early, 40–60 cm later.
- Allowable depletion (rule of thumb): irrigate when ~30–50% of available water is used.

Field method:
- Dig a small hole 20–30 cm deep.
- Squeeze soil: if it forms a weak ball and crumbles, you’re near the irrigation threshold.
- If it forms a sticky ribbon, it may be too wet.

## 2.4 Crop stage matters (example: tomato)
- After transplanting (first 7–10 days): frequent small irrigations to reduce stress.
- Vegetative growth: steady moisture; avoid big swings.
- Flowering/fruit set: water stress can reduce fruit set; prioritize consistent irrigation.
- Ripening: slightly reduce water to limit cracking, but do not let plants wilt.

## 2.5 Drip irrigation vs sprinkler
Drip delivers water to roots and keeps leaves dry, reducing fungal disease (early blight, downy mildew). Sprinklers cool the canopy but increase leaf wetness and disease risk.

## 2.6 Salinity and “white crust”
High salinity water can leave white crust on soil surface. Signs include poor germination and leaf burn on edges. Management:
- Use drip to concentrate water near roots.
- Occasionally apply a leaching irrigation if drainage is adequate.
- Mulch to reduce evaporation (evaporation leaves salts behind).

## 2.7 Simple irrigation recipe for sandy soil vegetables
If you have sandy soil and drip lines:
- Start with 20–30 minutes per day (or per irrigation cycle), then adjust using plant response.
- In hot windy days, split into 2 cycles (morning + late afternoon) to reduce deep percolation loss.
- Watch leaves at midday: mild midday droop can be normal; severe droop that continues into evening means under-watering.

## 2.8 Weather notes for coastal Mediterranean spring
In spring, temperature swings are common. Wind can increase evapotranspiration even if temperature is moderate. After a heat spike, plants can suddenly need more water.

## 2.9 Record keeping
Write down irrigation duration, date, and any rainfall. This helps calibrate your schedule to your field.
