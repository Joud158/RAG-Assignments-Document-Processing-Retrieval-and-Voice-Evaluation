# Chapter 5 — Farm Business Decisions Under Price Volatility

## 5.1 The reality
When currency and input prices change quickly, farmers face uncertainty. A “good” agronomic decision can still be a bad financial decision if the market price drops or inputs spike.

## 5.2 Build a simple cost sheet (one page)
Track costs per dunum or per hectare:
- Seeds/seedlings
- Fertilizers
- Pesticides
- Water and pumping
- Labor
- Transport and packaging

Then estimate expected yield and selling price range (low / medium / high). This gives you a break-even price.

## 5.3 Example: deciding whether to buy a fungicide
Ask:
1) How much yield is at risk without treatment?
2) What is the probability the treatment works (timing + diagnosis)?
3) What is the all-in cost (product + labor + fuel)?
4) Are there cheaper alternatives (leaf removal, spacing, irrigation change)?

Simple decision rule:
- Treat only if (expected saved revenue) > (cost of treatment) and resistance risk is acceptable.
Expected saved revenue ≈ (yield at risk) × (market price) × (probability of success).

## 5.4 Managing exchange-rate risk (practical habits)
- Keep records in both LBP and USD equivalent at purchase date.
- If possible, buy high-impact inputs early for the season when prices are favorable.
- Use group purchasing (co-ops) to reduce unit cost and improve negotiating power.

## 5.5 Pricing your produce
If your costs are volatile, consider:
- Pre-agreeing on price ranges with buyers.
- Diversifying crops so one market crash doesn’t wipe you out.
- Adding value (washing, grading, packaging) when it increases price more than it increases cost.

## 5.6 Working with NGOs and support programs
Some programs require evidence: field records, photos, receipts, and yield estimates. Good documentation increases your chances of support.

## 5.7 Summary
Good farming is both biology and economics. Use data to reduce uncertainty.
